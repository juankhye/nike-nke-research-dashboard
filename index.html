01 — HOW NIKE WORKS

The business model, from first principles

Nike is not a factory. It is a brand and a design house that rents the world's manufacturing capacity, then sells the output two ways — through other stores (wholesale) and its own channels (direct). Understanding that split is the key to the whole story.

Explain like I know nothing A sportswear company earns money on the gap between what a shoe costs to make and what someone pays for it. Nike widens that gap two ways: (1) brand — spending billions on athletes, teams and marketing (called demand creationNike's term for its advertising & sponsorship budget — the money spent to create demand for product (~$4.8B in FY26, ~10% of sales).) so people pay full price; and (2) channel — selling directly to you (a Nike store or the app) keeps the retailer's cut. It designs the product, hands specs to independent factories in Asia, then ships to two types of buyer.

Why the channel mix is the debate

Under the prior CEO, Nike bet the company on going direct — pulling product from wholesalers to sell it itself at full margin via Nike.com and the SNKRSNike's sneaker-launch app for hyped/limited releases — a pillar of its digital DTC strategy. app. It backfired: shelves at Foot Locker and Dick's emptied, competitors filled the gap, and Nike's own digital sales became discount-driven. sell-in & sell-through

The jargon that runs the story

  • Sell-in vs sell-through — Sell-in = Nike shipping to a retailer. Sell-through = the retailer selling to you. When sell-in outruns sell-through, inventory piles up and discounts follow — Nike's problem for two years.
  • Franchise management — Controlling supply of iconic shoes (Air Force 1, Dunk) so they stay scarce and desirable, rather than flooding the market.
  • ASP & markdowns — Average selling price; markdowns are discounts. Full-price selling is the health signal.
  • Lifestyle vs Performance — "Lifestyle/Sportswear" = fashion sneakers you wear on the street (~58% of Nike Brand). "Performance" = actual running/basketball/football gear. The turnaround is a deliberate tilt back toward performance.
02 — THE TURNAROUND

"Win Now" → "Sport Offense": resetting a brand on purpose

Nike is deliberately shrinking today to grow tomorrow. It is choking off the over-supplied classic sneakers that were quietly cannibalising the brand, rebuilding retail relationships, and re-centering on sport. Management calls it "quality over quantity" — which means two more quarters of falling sales before growth returns.

The core idea Nike got hooked on three retro shoes — the Air Force 1, Dunk, and Air Jordan 1. They were cheap to make and sold themselves, so Nike pumped out more and more. Eventually they were everywhere, discounted, and no longer cool. Hill's team is intentionally pulling ~$2 billion of these out of the market (a ~4-point drag on FY26 sales) to make them scarce again — while launching fresh performance product to fill the hole.

CFO transition: why an outsider matters

Long-time CFO Matt Friend hands over to Dave Denton (ex-CFO of Pfizer, Lowe's and CVS) on Aug 17, 2026. Nike's 8-K is explicit that the exit was not due to any disagreement. The signal is twofold: (1) hiring an external, large-cap operator breaks Nike's insider tradition and prioritises capital discipline as it pivots from turnaround to growth; and (2) Denton's $4.0M performance award cliff-vests Dec 2027 purely on FY27 operating-margin expansion — his incentives are bolted to the margin-recovery thesis. His Tapestry board seat during Coach's turnaround is the relevant analogue.

Why China went from moat to millstone

Domestic share loss

Local champions Anta and Li-Ning — vertically integrated and faster to market — plus Western niche brands (On, Arc'teryx, Salomon) are taking share. Nationalist sentiment favours home brands.

Deliberate reset

Nike is intentionally cutting low-quality, promo-led sell-in, exiting the 6.18/11.11 discount events, and refusing to match local price points — sacrificing revenue for marketplace health.

Green shoots

100+ remodeled doors (of ~5,000) show positive traffic and comps; a local product-creation leader and coverage across ~20 cities. Sell-out positive, but sell-in still negative.

04 — SUPPLY CHAIN & TARIFFS

Where it's actually made — and why tariffs matter

Nike owns virtually no factories. Independent contractors in a handful of Asian countries make everything, then ship it — increasingly into the United States, where import tariffs land directly on gross margin.

Explain like I know nothing When a shoe made in Vietnam is imported into the US, the importer (Nike) pays a tariff — a tax that's a % of the shoe's cost. Nike sources ~half its footwear from Vietnam and most apparel there too, so a jump in US tariffs is a direct hit to profit. In FY26 a one-time $986M refund of previously-paid tariffs flattered the numbers; in FY27 tariffs flip back to a ~220bps headwind that Nike must offset with pricing and cost cuts.

06 — COMPETITIVE MOAT

What protects Nike — and where the wall is cracking

Nike's moat is brand, scale, marketing firepower, athlete relationships and innovation. All five are real. All five are under more pressure than at any point in a decade, as challenger brands take the premium high ground.

Where the moat is cracking

  • Premium running: On, Hoka, and a resurgent New Balance & Asics have taken the high-end; RBC says Nike "surrendered price leadership."
  • Retro/court lifestyle: Adidas (Samba, Spezial, Gazelle) owns the trend Nike used to.
  • Premium women's: Lululemon, Vuori, Alo are taking share Nike barely contests.
  • The "superpower": UBS argues Nike is losing its "all things to all people" status as it drifts down-market (Kohl's, DSW, Academy, Amazon).

Where it still wins

  • Running comeback: 5 straight quarters of double-digit growth, +5pts share; Vomero/Pegasus/Structure re-platformed.
  • Football & the World Cup: Mercurial/Tiempo/Phantom all refreshed; AeroFIT kits selling 2.5× the 2022 pace (2026 World Cup is in North America).
  • Women's signature: Sabrina, A'ja, and a Caitlin Clark signature launching Fall 2026.
  • Jordan resale premium: still among the highest secondary-market values in sport.
Is the moat widening or narrowing? Honest read from the research: narrowing, but not breached. Nike has clearly lost ground in premium running, lifestyle-retro and China, and its basketball-culture engine has cooled. But no rival has Nike's scale, marketing budget or breadth — and the running/innovation rebound shows the machine still works when pointed at sport. The bear case is that "loyal to trend, not brand" fashion customers have simply moved on; the bull case is that a re-focused Nike re-widens the moat from here.

08 — VALUATION

What's priced in at ~$41?

On trough earnings, Nike optically looks expensive (~20× FY26 EPS). The bull case is that you're paying ~16× a recovering FY27 and buying a normalisation to ~$2.70–3.00 EPS. The bear case is that ~20× a broken-model trough is not cheap. The Street can't agree — targets run from $42 to $75.

That's the crux: if you believe Nike normalises to ~$2.70–3.00 of EPS and a low-double-digit margin (Bernstein/Jefferies), the stock is materially undervalued. 10 — KEY RISKS

Ranked risks & what to monitor

The five things most likely to break the thesis, ordered by how much they matter to the debate today.

1 · Greater China High
Structural share loss to Anta/Li-Ning and a consumer that favours local brands. −13% cc in FY26 with no bottom called. Was Nike's highest-margin growth engine.
Watch: Pou Sheng monthly sales, reset-door comps, sell-in vs sell-through gap.
2 · Competition / brand heat High
On, Hoka, New Balance, Adidas taking premium share. "Loyal to trend, not brand" fashion buyers have drifted away. Nike ceded running price leadership.
Watch: Running share, new-franchise sell-through, resale premiums, competitor cc growth.
3 · Tariffs / supply chain Medium
~50% of footwear from Vietnam; a 10%→15% US tariff = ~220bps FY27 headwind. FY26's $986M refund won't repeat. Concentrated sourcing = fragility.
Watch: US tariff-rate changes, Section 301, mitigation progress, freight/oil costs.
4 · Wholesale reset execution Medium
Re-entering wholesale from a position of weakness on worse terms; must avoid re-flooding channels while regaining shelf. Margin vs reach trade-off.
Watch: Foot Locker/Dick's order books, Amazon volume discipline, DTC stabilisation.
5 · Consumer / K-shaped spend Medium
Demand decelerated in May on soft discretionary spending. Low-income cohorts (where mass Nike also plays) are pulling back; strength concentrated in affluent shoppers.
Watch: Card-spend proxies, traffic, markdown depth, back-to-school.
6 · Inventory / markdown Lower
Inventory is now clean (~$7.5B, flat) — the least acute risk. The danger is a demand air-pocket forcing renewed clearance and undoing full-price progress.
Watch: Inventory growth vs sales, off-price mix, gross-margin bridge.
